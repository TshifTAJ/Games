📐 How the AI Thinks — Plain English Guide

The Mufuvha AI uses a technique called Reinforcement Learning (RL) — the same family of methods that powers AlphaGo, chess engines, and robots. This page explains every formula and component in simple, everyday language. No maths degree needed.

🎯 The Big Idea: Q-Values

Q(s, a) is the AI's score estimate for a move. The letter Q stands for "quality." Before every move, the AI asks: "If I pick pit a when the board looks like s, how good is that choice in the long run?"

The answer — the Q-value — is just a single number. A high Q-value means "this looks like a great move." A low or negative Q-value means "this is probably bad." The AI always picks the move with the highest Q-value (unless it's still exploring — more on that below).

Everyday analogy: Imagine rating each pit on a scale of −10 to +10 based on how much you expect to win by picking it. Q-learning is the AI constantly updating those ratings as it plays more games.

🔢 What is φ (phi) — The Feature Vector?

φ(s') is a list of 50 numbers that describe the board after a move. Think of it as the AI's checklist of board facts. Each number (feature) captures one aspect of the position.

Features 1–8: Your inner row
How many seeds are in each of your 8 front pits, divided by total seeds on board (normalised). High = good coverage.
Features 9–16: Your outer row
How many seeds are in your 8 back pits. Outer seeds are safe from capture — these are your reserves.
Features 17–24: Opponent's inner row
How loaded the opponent's front pits are. Highly loaded opposite pits are capture opportunities.
Features 25–32: Opponent's outer row
Opponent's reserves — capturing both inner and outer in a column is a "double capture."
Features 33–34: Capture scores
Your captures ÷ 64 and opponent's captures ÷ 64. Tells the AI who is winning.
Features 35–50: Occupied flags
Binary 1/0 — is each inner pit occupied? Lets the AI reason about capture opportunities directly.
Why 50 features? There are 264 possible board states — more than the number of atoms in your body. You can't store a Q-value for every one. Instead, 50 numbers summarise the board well enough to make smart decisions in any situation.

⚖️ What is w — The Weight Vector?

w is a list of 50 numbers — the AI's learned importance scores. Each weight corresponds to one feature in φ. A large positive weight means "this feature strongly predicts winning." A negative weight means "this feature predicts trouble."

At the start, all weights are zero — the AI knows nothing. As it plays, weights are nudged up or down after every move based on how well things went. After thousands of games, the weights encode a complete strategy.

Everyday analogy: Think of w as a recipe. Each ingredient (feature) has an amount (weight). Too much of a particular ingredient ruins the dish. Q-learning adjusts the recipe after every meal until the dish consistently tastes good.

📊 Formula 1: Q(s, a) ≈ w · φ(s')

Q(s, a) ≈ w · φ(s')

What this means in plain English:

To score a move, the AI takes each of the 50 features, multiplies it by its corresponding weight, and adds them all up. That sum is the Q-value — the AI's prediction of how good this move is.

Step by step:
1. The AI imagines making move a on the current board s
2. It reads off the 50 features of the resulting position → that's φ(s')
3. It multiplies feature[1] × weight[1], feature[2] × weight[2] … all 50 pairs
4. It adds them all together → that sum is Q(s, a)
5. It repeats for every legal pit, then picks the pit with the highest Q
Why a dot product? A dot product (w · φ) is simply multiply-then-add for two lists. It's the fastest possible way to turn 50 board facts into one decision number. This is called linear function approximation.

🏅 What is r — The Reward?

r is the immediate score the AI receives after making a move. Think of it as points:

🌱
Capture move
+0.05 × number of seeds captured
🏆
Win the game
+1.0 (a big bonus)
❌
Lose the game
−1.0 (a penalty)
➡️
Normal move
0 (no immediate score)
Why are non-capture moves worth 0? The AI learns the value of strategic positioning through future rewards, not just immediate captures. The γ (gamma) factor below handles this.

⚡ Formula 2: δ = r + γ·maxQ' − Q(s,a) — The "Surprise" (TD Error)

δ = r + γ · maxQ' − Q(s,a)

What this means in plain English:

δ (delta) measures how surprised the AI was by what actually happened. It compares what the AI expected to happen with what actually happened — and the difference is the learning signal.

r — Immediate reward
What you earned right now. Seeds captured this move, or win/loss signal at game end.
γ · maxQ' — Future value
γ (gamma = 0.95) discounts future rewards. maxQ' is the best Q-value in the new position. Together they ask: "how good is the situation I've arrived in?"
−Q(s,a) — What was expected
The AI's original prediction before making the move. Subtracting it tells us: was reality better or worse than expected?
δ > 0
Positive surprise! Reality was better than expected. The AI should do this more.
δ < 0
Negative surprise. Reality was worse than expected. The AI should avoid this.
δ ≈ 0
No surprise. The AI predicted correctly. Weights stay almost unchanged.
γ = 0.95 explained: The AI values a reward one step in the future at 95% of its face value, two steps away at 95%² = 90.25%, and so on. This makes it care about the near future more than the far future — exactly like a real player who plans a few moves ahead.

🔄 Formula 3: w ← w + α·δ·φ(s') — The Learning Step

w ← w + α · δ · φ(s')

What this means in plain English:

This is the line where actual learning happens. After every AI move, we update all 50 weights simultaneously based on the surprise signal δ. Weights that were responsible for the move get nudged in the direction of the surprise.

α (alpha) = 0.01
The learning rate. Only 1% of the error signal is applied each step. Too large → AI overshoots and forgets what it learned. Too small → learning is painfully slow.
δ (delta)
The surprise from Formula 2. If δ is large and positive, weights get a big boost. If δ is large and negative, weights get reduced.
φ(s') — features
Only features that were non-zero (i.e. relevant to the move) get updated. If a feature wasn't a factor in the decision, its weight doesn't change.
Everyday analogy: Imagine coaching a student after a test. The mistake (δ) tells you what to fix. The revision effort (α) is how hard you drill each concept. The relevant topics (φ) are what you focus on. You only revise concepts that appeared on that test — not everything.

🎲 ε-Greedy Exploration: How the AI Decides Whether to Try Something New

The AI faces a dilemma every move: try something new and possibly discover a better strategy (explore), or use the best strategy it already knows (exploit). ε (epsilon) is the probability of exploring.

🎲 Explore (random move)
Happens with probability ε. The AI picks a random legal pit. This discovers new strategies it might never find otherwise. Essential early in training when ε = 1.0 (always exploring).
🧠 Exploit (best known move)
Happens with probability 1−ε. The AI picks the pit with the highest Q-value — its current best answer. As ε shrinks toward 0.05, the AI plays more confidently using what it has learned.

Every time the AI makes a move, ε is multiplied by 0.9994. Starting at 1.0 and decaying slowly, ε eventually levels off at 0.05 (5% random). The AI gradually shifts from full exploration to confident exploitation.

Everyday analogy: You're learning to cook. At first you try random recipes (high ε). Over time you make the recipes you've found taste best (low ε). But you always keep a 5% chance of trying something new — just in case there's a better dish you haven't discovered yet.

🔁 The Complete 4-Step Learning Loop

1️⃣
Observe the board
Read all 50 features → this is the state s. This is the AI "seeing" the board.
2️⃣
Choose a move
ε-greedy: explore (random) or exploit (highest Q). The AI picks pit a.
3️⃣
Get feedback
Execute the move. Collect reward r. Observe new state s'. Compute δ (the surprise).
4️⃣
Update weights
Apply w ← w + α·δ·φ. Decay ε. The AI is now slightly smarter. Repeat from step 1.

This loop runs for every single move of every game — potentially tens of thousands of times during training. After 2,000 training episodes, the AI has seen enough situations to play strategically.

🏗️ The TEP Framework (Mitchell, 1997) — How We Measure Learning

Mitchell's framework defines a machine learning problem with three components. The left side panel (TEP Live Monitor) shows these in real time during play.

T — Task

What is the AI trying to do? Win at Mufuvha by selecting moves that maximise captured seeds. Each legal move is an action in the task.

E — Experience

How does it learn? Through self-play — it plays thousands of games against itself, observing which moves lead to wins and losses. Every move is a data point.

P — Performance

How do we measure improvement? Win rate against a random player (Easy AI) and a heuristic player (Medium AI), measured every 200 training episodes.

About Mufuvha (Mefuvha)

Mufuvha (also Mefuvha, meaning "foggy rain" in Tshivenda) is an indigenous four-row Mancala strategy game of Limpopo Province, South Africa, closely related to Moruba (Pedi) and Tsoro (Shona). Played by the Venda people on solid wooden boards carved from tree trunks — some measuring two meters with up to 28 holes per row, making it one of the largest Mancala games ever documented.

Featured at the 2004 inaugural indigenous games festival at Peter Mokaba Stadium, Polokwane. The "Story of Sankhambi and the Baboons" — a legend about the game — was documented by ethnologist Hugh Stayt in 1931. Traditionally prohibited after sunset (believed to provoke cattle theft); fruit pips, not stones, used during the rainy season (to avoid turning rain to hail).

🌱 Educational legacy: Mancala is one of the most researched traditional games in the world. Hundreds of schools across Europe and the USA run Mancala tournaments to teach mathematical skills and intercultural awareness. Mufuvha specifically connects South African learners to their own intellectual heritage.

Authentic Rules (Researched)

  • Board: 4 rows × 8 holes. Each player controls inner + outer row.
  • Setup: 2 seeds per hole, except leftmost inner = 0, next = 1.
  • Move: Pick any non-empty hole in either of your rows.
  • Relay: Last seed in occupied pit → pick up and continue sowing.
  • Capture: Last seed in empty inner pit, opposite non-empty opponent inner → capture inner + outer same column.
  • Win: Opponent has no seeds. Most captures when board exhausted.

TEP Framework (Mitchell, 1997)

Task (T)

Select moves to capture all opponent seeds. 1–16 legal moves per turn; relay chains create deep horizons.

Experience (E)

Self-play. Agent plays both sides; updates on P1 turns. +1 win, −1 loss, +0.05×n captured.

Performance (P)

Win rate vs Random and Heuristic every 200 episodes, 60 eval games, ε=0.

⚡ How to Amplify Learning — For Players, Parents & Teachers

🗣 Think Out Loud

Say your strategy as you play: "I'm picking this pit because my last seed will land on an empty pit facing their loaded inner row." Verbalising reasoning deepens understanding — this is metacognition.

📓 Keep a Game Journal

After each game, write one thing you did well and one thing you would change. Reflection converts experience into learning. This habit transfers to school and work performance.

🎓 Teach Someone Else

The fastest way to master Mufuvha is to teach it. When you explain the rules and strategy to a younger sibling or friend, you discover gaps in your own understanding and fill them.

🔢 Count Everything

Before every move, count all seeds in every relevant pit — don't estimate. Make mental arithmetic a game within the game. Time yourself. Speed and accuracy are both skills.

🤖 Analyse the AI

After losing to the AI, replay the game mentally. At what point did the AI gain the advantage? What move did you miss? Analysing AI decisions develops algorithmic thinking.

🏆 Run a Tournament

Teachers: organise classroom Mufuvha tournaments. Competition builds motivation, sportsmanship, and social skills. Design a leaderboard and give certificates for "Best Strategy" not just winning.

🌍 Explore Variants

Change the board settings (Settings → Board Rules). Try 6 columns with 3 seeds each. How does the strategy change? Experimenting with rules builds systems thinking and adaptability.

📐 Connect to School

Maths teachers: use Mufuvha to teach sequences, patterns, and graph theory. Life Orientation: use it to teach decision-making. History: connect it to Venda culture and the 2004 indigenous games festival.

🧘 Mindful Moves

Challenge yourself to make zero impulsive moves. Breathe before each turn. This mindfulness practice improves focus and links Mufuvha to proven stress-reduction techniques.
